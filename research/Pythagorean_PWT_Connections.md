## 3. Connections to Prime Number Theory

### 3.1 Φ and Primes — The Structural Bond

The Fibonacci sequence F(n), whose consecutive ratios converge to Φ, has deep prime entanglement:

- **Divisibility:** Every prime p divides some Fibonacci number. Specifically, p | F(p−1) or p | F(p+1) (depending on p mod 5).
- **Fibonacci primes:** F(n) is prime only if n is prime (necessary but not sufficient) — primality is a prerequisite for Fibonacci primality.
- **Lucas primality tests:** Lucas sequences (built on Φ) are the basis of the Lucas-Lehmer test for Mersenne primes, one of the most powerful primality tests known.
- **Zeckendorf's theorem:** Every positive integer has a unique representation as a sum of non-consecutive Fibonacci numbers — an alternative "base-Φ" decomposition paralleling prime factorisation.

Wheeler's framework suggests Φ is not merely a ratio but the *generative principle* of manifestation. If so, primes — as the irreducible atoms of arithmetic — would naturally emerge from Φ-structured processes.

The incommensurable triangle's defining property is **fractal self-similarity**: subdivide it and the part is identical to the whole. Prime distribution exhibits analogous behaviour:

- **Scaling invariance:** The prime counting function π(x) ~ x/ln(x) shows that primes "thin out" logarithmically, but the *local* distribution at any scale resembles the global pattern — Cramér's model treats prime gaps as locally random with globally predictable statistics.
- **The Ulam spiral:** Primes plotted on a square spiral cluster along diagonal lines (quadratic polynomials). This is an empirical self-similarity — the pattern at small scale echoes at large scale.
- **Benford's law for primes:** The distribution of leading digits of primes follows logarithmic patterns reminiscent of golden-ratio scaling.

The 108-36-36° triangle may offer a geometric language for describing this: primes as the "incommensurable cuts" of the number line, each one producing a smaller copy of the overall distribution pattern.
