## 3. The Five Human Senses and Their Spectral Domains

### 3.1 Sight — Electromagnetic Radiation
- **Range:** ~380–750 nm (400–789 THz)
- **Detector:** Retinal photoreceptors (rods, 3 types of cones)
- **Mechanism:** Photon absorption → cis-trans isomerisation of retinal
- **Bandwidth:** ~1 octave of EM spectrum

### 3.2 Hearing — Mechanical Pressure Waves
- **Range:** ~20 Hz – 20 kHz (acoustic, not EM)
- **Detector:** Cochlear hair cells (tonotopically arranged)
- **Mechanism:** Basilar membrane resonance → mechanotransduction
- **Bandwidth:** ~10 octaves (2¹⁰ ≈ 1000:1 ratio)
- **Note:** Sound is NOT electromagnetic — it's longitudinal pressure waves in matter

### 3.3 Touch — Mechanical Pressure + Thermal (IR)
- **Pressure:** DC to ~1 kHz vibrotactile
- **Thermal:** Skin responds to IR radiation (~3–50 μm, peak sensitivity ~10 μm = 30 THz)
- **Detectors:** Meissner (light touch), Pacinian (vibration), Ruffini (stretch), Merkel (pressure), free nerve endings (pain/temp)
- **Thermal note:** We literally feel infrared radiation — hold hand near a hot object without touching it

### 3.4 Smell — Molecular Shape + Vibration
- **Traditional model:** Lock-and-key (molecular shape fits receptor)
- **Vibrational theory (Luca Turin):** Olfactory receptors may detect molecular vibration frequencies via inelastic electron tunnelling
  - If true, smell operates in the **IR range** (~1–100 THz, wavenumbers ~33–3333 cm⁻¹)
  - This would make smell a kind of **biological spectroscopy**
- **Frequency range of molecular vibrations:** ~10¹² – 10¹⁴ Hz (THz to low-IR)
- **Status:** Controversial but not disproven. Some evidence shows humans can distinguish isotopes (same shape, different vibration)

### 3.5 Taste — Chemical/Electrical
- **Modalities:** Sweet, sour, salty, bitter, umami (+ fat, possibly more)
- **Mechanism:** Chemical binding to G-protein coupled receptors (sweet/bitter/umami) or ion channels (sour/salty)
- **Not directly frequency-based** — operates on molecular recognition
- **Indirect EM connection:** Molecular binding involves electron orbital interactions at optical/UV frequencies

## 4. Key Analysis: Colour-Music Correspondence

### 4.1 Visible Light Ratios vs Musical Intervals

Mapping the 7 colour centre frequencies as ratios from red:

| Colour  | Ratio from Red | Nearest musical interval | Just ratio | Match? |
|---------|---------------|--------------------------|------------|--------|
| Red     | 1.000         | Unison (C)               | 1.000      | ✓      |
| Orange  | 1.127         | Major 2nd (D)            | 1.125      | **≈ YES** (0.2% off) |
| Yellow  | 1.180         | Minor 3rd?               | 1.200      | ~close |
| Green   | 1.282         | Major 3rd (E)            | 1.250      | ~2.5% off |
| Blue    | 1.436         | Perfect 4th? (F)         | 1.333      | ✗ off by 8% |
| Indigo  | 1.566         | Perfect 5th? (G)         | 1.500      | ~4% off |
| Violet  | 1.705         | Major 6th? (A)           | 1.667      | ~2% off |

**Verdict:** The match is **suggestive but imprecise**. Red→Orange maps beautifully to a major second. Green approximates a major third. But the middle and upper colours deviate significantly from clean musical intervals.

### 4.2 Newton's Colour-Music Mapping — Genius or Forced?

Newton explicitly mapped his 7 colours to the 7 notes of the Dorian mode:

| Newton's colour | Newton's note |
|----------------|---------------|
| Red            | D             |
| Orange         | E             |
| Yellow         | F             |
| Green          | G             |
| Blue           | A             |
| Indigo         | B             |
| Violet         | C             |

**Historical context:**
- Newton originally saw **5** colours in the spectrum (like most people)
- He **added** orange and indigo specifically to make 7, to match the musical scale
- He was deeply influenced by Pythagorean mysticism and the idea of cosmic harmony
- The choice of 7 was **partly forced** by his desire for musical correspondence

**However:**
- The fact that visible light spans almost exactly one octave is **NOT something Newton forced** — it's a physical fact about human photoreceptor sensitivity
- Newton's intuition that frequency ratios matter was **correct in principle**
- The specific colour-note mapping doesn't hold up to precise measurement

**Assessment: Newton was ~60% right.** The octave structure is real. The detailed mapping is forced.

### 4.3 The Visible Octave — Coincidence?

**The fact:** Human vision spans 400–789 THz ≈ 1.97:1 ≈ one octave.

**Arguments it's NOT coincidence:**
1. Three cone types (S, M, L) have peak sensitivities at ~420, ~530, ~560 nm
2. The ~2:1 ratio may relate to photochemistry constraints — the energy to break/isomerise retinal sets the lower bound, and UV damage sets the upper bound
3. Evolution may have "tuned" to one octave because it provides maximum information with minimum bandwidth
4. Water absorption: water is transparent in exactly this range, creating an evolutionary pressure window

**Arguments it IS coincidence:**
1. The boundaries are somewhat arbitrary (sensitivity trails off gradually)
2. Different definitions of "visible" give ratios from 1.7 to 2.1
3. Many animals see outside this range (bees see UV, snakes see IR)

**Best assessment:** It's a **constrained coincidence**. Physics (water transparency window, solar spectrum peak, photochemistry) constrains the range, and one octave happens to be about the right bandwidth for useful colour discrimination. The 2:1 ratio is approximate, not exact.

### 5.3 Ratio Analysis — Are There Patterns?

**Observation 1: Radio sub-bands are uniform decades (×10)**

Each radio sub-band (ELF→SLF→ULF→VLF→LF→MF→HF→VHF→UHF→SHF→EHF) spans exactly **one decade** (ratio 10:1 = ~3.32 octaves). This is purely a human convention (ITU designation), not physics.

**Observation 2: The physically meaningful bands have irregular ratios**

The transitions between IR, visible, UV, X-ray, and gamma are set by **physical processes**, not convention:
- IR/visible boundary: photoreceptor chemistry
- UV/X-ray boundary: atomic ionisation energies
- X-ray/gamma boundary: nuclear energy scales

These don't follow neat decimal or musical ratios.

**Observation 3: Powers of 10 dominate, but...**

The full EM spectrum from radio (10³ Hz) to gamma (10²⁰ Hz) spans ~17 decades. The major boundaries fall roughly at:

```
Radio:      10³  – 10⁹   Hz  (6 decades)
Microwave:  10⁹  – 10¹¹  Hz  (2 decades) 
IR:         10¹¹ – 10¹⁴  Hz  (3 decades)
Visible:    10¹⁴·⁶ – 10¹⁴·⁹ Hz (0.3 decades)
UV:         10¹⁴·⁹ – 10¹⁶·⁵ Hz (1.6 decades)
X-ray:      10¹⁶·⁵ – 10¹⁹·⁵ Hz (3 decades)
Gamma:      >10¹⁹·⁵ Hz
```

Decade widths: **6, 2, 3, 0.3, 1.6, 3, ...**

These don't obviously map to prime sequences, musical ratios, or clean mathematical patterns. The widths correlate with **how many distinct physical mechanisms operate** in each range.

## 7. Patterns Potentially Useful for Circuit Design

### 7.1 The Octave Principle

If visible light (a system evolution optimised for maximum information extraction) spans one octave, then **any sensing/processing circuit might be optimally designed around one-octave bandwidth**.

For a circuit operating at baseband:
- **1 MHz – 2 MHz** = one octave
- **10 MHz – 20 MHz** = one octave
- **100 Hz – 200 Hz** = one octave (audio applications)

### 7.2 Musical Ratio Frequencies

If you're designing a multi-frequency circuit (e.g., for QPf harmonic processing), consider frequencies at just-intonation ratios from a base frequency f₀:

| Ratio | Factor | If f₀ = 1 MHz | If f₀ = 10 kHz | Musical interval |
|-------|--------|---------------|-----------------|------------------|
| 1/1   | 1.000  | 1.000 MHz     | 10.000 kHz      | Unison           |
| 9/8   | 1.125  | 1.125 MHz     | 11.250 kHz      | Major 2nd        |
| 5/4   | 1.250  | 1.250 MHz     | 12.500 kHz      | Major 3rd        |
| 4/3   | 1.333  | 1.333 MHz     | 13.333 kHz      | Perfect 4th      |
| 3/2   | 1.500  | 1.500 MHz     | 15.000 kHz      | Perfect 5th      |

| 15/8  | 1.875  | 1.875 MHz     | 18.750 kHz      | Major 7th        |
| 2/1   | 2.000  | 2.000 MHz     | 20.000 kHz      | Octave           |

**Why this matters for circuits:**
- These ratios produce **minimal intermodulation distortion** in nonlinear systems
- Harmonics of these frequencies tend to **reinforce rather than interfere**
- LC circuits tuned to these ratios would produce standing wave patterns analogous to musical consonance
- If QPf structures use prime-factor decomposition, the just-intonation ratios (built from primes 2, 3, 5) are natural candidates

### 7.3 The Prime Triad: 2, 3, 5

All musical consonance reduces to three primes: **2, 3, and 5**.

| Prime | Musical role              | Physical role                    | Circuit analogy          |
|-------|--------------------------|----------------------------------|--------------------------|
| 2     | Octave (doubling)        | Frequency doubling / halving     | Binary division, flip-flops |
| 3     | Perfect fifth (×3/2)     | Third harmonic, triangle waves   | Divide-by-3, ring counters |
| 5     | Major third (×5/4)       | Fifth harmonic                   | Less common but appears in pentatonic scales |

**Design principle:** A circuit whose operating frequencies relate by factors of 2, 3, and 5 (and their products) will exhibit harmonic coherence — signals reinforce rather than create noise.

### 7.4 EM Band Scaling for Circuit Design

If we compress the EM spectrum pattern into a practical circuit range:

| EM band analogy | Ratio from base | At f₀ = 100 Hz | Function              |
|-----------------|----------------|-----------------|-----------------------|
| "Radio"         | ×1             | 100 Hz          | Base clock            |

The ratios between these are: **100, 1000, 100, 30** — not particularly musical. This suggests EM band boundaries are set by **physical mechanism changes**, not universal ratios.

### 7.5 Logarithmic Spacing — The Key Pattern

The one truly universal pattern across both EM spectrum and music:

> **Perception and physical organisation are logarithmic, not linear.**

- Musical pitch: logarithmic (each octave is ×2)
- Colour perception: approximately logarithmic across the visible range
- EM band designations: logarithmic (decades)
- Weber-Fechner law: all human senses respond logarithmically to stimulus intensity
- Electronic decades: RF engineering naturally works in dB (logarithmic)

**The circuit design takeaway:** Design frequency plans on **logarithmic scales**. Space operating frequencies geometrically (constant ratios), not arithmetically (constant differences).
